Practice

Four practice areas, one underlying craft.

Instead of a list of roles, this is a portrait of the work itself. Each section below describes a practice area in which I have done sustained, deep work over many years, the kinds of problems it lets me solve, and the tools I reach for when solving them.

Practice 01

Generative AI for Network Operations

Current focus

Enterprise wireless networks generate a constant stream of telemetry that is, on its own, very hard to act on. A typical deployment runs across thousands of access points and switches, each producing measurements about radio conditions, client behaviour, roaming events, throughput, latency and a long tail of device-health indicators. When something goes wrong, the question is rarely what changed, but which of the many things that changed actually matters, and that requires reasoning across modalities, time horizons and levels of abstraction.

The systems I build in this area combine large language model reasoning with classical statistical signal processing and a knowledge graph of the network domain. The language model is never asked to interpret raw telemetry by itself. Instead, the architecture is arranged so that statistical models identify anomalies, the knowledge graph supplies structure (devices, sites, topology, configuration), and the language model is used for the parts of the problem that language models are genuinely good at, namely synthesising context, generating hypotheses and explaining findings in a way a human operator can act on.

The work has produced autonomous agent workflows that monitor networks continuously and propose remediation plans, multi-modal correlation across telemetry, device-health indicators and client performance data, and network-aware inference pipelines that evaluate anomalies over time to reduce false alerts. In production these tools have collapsed wireless-issue investigation times from hours to seconds and have given product and engineering teams a richer surface on which to act.

What makes this work distinctive is the discipline around grounding. A generative model that hallucinates is not a quirk to be tolerated, it is a defect to be designed out. I treat retrieval, structured reasoning, and probabilistic evaluation as first-class members of the system, alongside the language model itself.

Practice 02

Probabilistic Modelling for Industrial Decisions

Foundation

Most industrial decision problems are problems of incomplete information. Sensors fail. Labels are noisy or absent. The distribution shifts between training time and deployment. In these settings the right object to model is not a point prediction but a distribution over possible answers, with calibrated uncertainty, and an explicit account of the assumptions baked into the model.

I have spent a decade building probabilistic models for problems of this kind, using PyMC3 for Bayesian inference in production, BLOG (Bayesian Logic) for relational probabilistic programming in security and access-control settings, and Swift for the more academic probabilistic graphical work I did during my post-doctoral years. The applications have ranged from Pareto-optimal alarm classification in commercial intrusion-detection systems, to root-cause analysis of event occurrences across distributed sites, to inferring the latent state of wireless clients from the partial observations available to a controller.

The thread across all of it is the same: when you model uncertainty explicitly, you can make decisions that are robust to that uncertainty. The system stops being surprised by its own confidence. Calibration becomes a property you measure rather than a property you hope for. And the conversation with product owners shifts from can we trust this prediction to what are we going to do given the prediction, and how much risk does that imply.

PyMC3 BLOG Swift PPL Probabilistic graphical models Influence diagrams Bayesian inference Clustering Regression analysis Pareto optimisation

Applied at Extreme Networks, Johnson Controls and the Insight Centre for Data Analytics.

Practice 03

Multi-Objective Optimisation and Preference Learning

Research line

Most decisions worth making involve several criteria that pull in different directions. Faster, but how much should we sacrifice in cost? More reliable, but at what loss of throughput? A perfectly calibrated model that gives one number cannot answer these questions. What is needed instead is an explicit account of the trade-off structure, a way to elicit preferences from the people who will live with the decision, and a class of algorithms that produce solutions which respect those preferences without committing to a single weighting in advance.

This was the subject of my doctorate at the National University of Ireland, Cork and the body of work I produced during my post-doctoral fellowship at the Insight Centre. The research line culminated in publications at AAAI, IJCAI, UAI, CP and ICTAI on multi-objective influence diagrams with possibly optimal policies, lexicographic and weighted-average preference inference, and multi-objective constraint optimisation with trade-offs, in collaboration with IBM Research Labs Dublin.

In industry, the same ideas appear under different names. When a wireless customer asks for a network that is reliable for their critical clients while being inexpensive to operate, that is a multi-objective decision problem. When a product team has to weigh connectivity reliability, roaming success, throughput stability and latency in a single recommendation, that is preference inference. The algorithms I developed for academic benchmarks transfer, with care, into the techniques I use today to personalise networking solutions and to evaluate client-experience metrics in a principled way.

Practice 04

Research to Production Engineering

The connecting craft

Many of the failures I have seen in applied AI are not failures of model quality but failures of translation between research code and production systems. A prototype that assumes a clean batch of data, a single GPU and unlimited iteration time will not survive contact with a streaming pipeline, a multi-tenant deployment and a service-level objective. Bridging that gap is its own craft, and it is the craft I most enjoy.

In practice, this means I write the same model twice, once in the language and shape of research and once in the language and shape of production. I work directly with the engineering and product teams that will own the system, so that the constraints they care about (latency, cost, failure modes, observability, retraining cadence) become inputs to the model design rather than afterthoughts. I prefer pipelines that I can reason about end to end, and I am sceptical of architectures whose correctness depends on assumptions that nobody on the team can explain in their own words.

The result, over many projects, is a set of next-generation real-time AI and machine learning pipelines that ship, scale and stay correct. The same care that I once applied to convergence proofs in my dissertation, I now apply to backpressure and idempotency in streaming jobs. The intellectual habits transfer, and the production work is the better for it.

Where this has been done

The organisations behind the work.

The practice areas above are not isolated to one job. They are threads I have woven through every role I have held, and the organisations below are the places I have done that weaving.

At Extreme Networks (since 2020, currently in the United States and previously in Cork) I have been responsible for the data-science strategy around generative AI for network diagnostics, multi-modal correlation of telemetry and client performance, and the personalisation of networking solutions to customer preferences. The work contributes to the next-generation real-time AI and machine-learning pipelines that underpin the company's enterprise networking platform.

At Johnson Controls (2017 to 2020, in Cork) I led machine-learning work across the intrusion-alarm, access-control and retail-analytics product lines. The eight US patents I co-invented during that period sit on top of probabilistic and rule-based models for alarm classification, security-system signature generation and event analysis.

At the Insight Centre for Data Analytics (2014 to 2017, in Cork) I held a Science Foundation Ireland Post-Doctoral Fellowship as part of a United Technology Research Centre machine-learning programme, with partial collaboration with IBM Research Labs Dublin. The work focused on probabilistic graphical models, clustering and decision making under uncertainty.

At the National University of Ireland, Cork (2010 to 2014) my doctoral research was funded by the IRCSET / IBM Enterprise Partnership Scheme and was part of one of three main streams of IBM's Risk Management Colloboratory project. I developed exact and approximate algorithms for multi-objective decision making under preference uncertainty, which became the research line that still informs my industrial work today.

Before all of that, I was a lecturer in mathematics at Osmania University in Hyderabad (2005 to 2010), teaching abstract and linear algebra, real and numerical analysis, differential and vector calculus, probability and statistics, and co-ordinate geometry to undergraduate students.
